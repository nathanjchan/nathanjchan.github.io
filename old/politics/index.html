Political Science

I follow politics pretty closely, although I try to completely avoid having partisan opinions. As a political science minor at UC Davis, I have taken a couple upper-division political science classes.

I took a class on the judicial interpretations of the First Amendment of the US Constitution. We looked at Supreme Court cases regarding the right to free expression, associational rights, and the right to free exercise of religious beliefs. I wrote two essays: one where I posed as a Supreme Court Justice writing an opinion on a hypothetical case, Keller v. Regents of the University of Califonia about free speech on a college campus, and one where I researched the actual case Janus v. AFSCME, and made a prediction on the outcome. My prediction was correct :)

I took another class concerning the theories of what constitutes a fair or just distribution of goods, especially economic goods. We read texts from four different philosophers and economists: John Rawls, Robert Nozick, John Roemer, and Friedrich Hayek. I wrote an essay that illustrates a dialogue between Rawls and Nozick, pitting against each other two different theories of distributive justice.

In high school, I wrote a couple essays, of which I am fairly proud. The first essay makes an argument about the brain's role in politics, and the second, not about politics, is about happiness.

As an active member of ASUCD (Associated Students, UC Davis), I have written several bills that affect ASUCD policy. The main one is a resolution I wrote as Legislative Director of the ASUCD Executive Office that made ASUCD adopt and endorse the Internaional Holocaust Remembrance Alliance's non-legally binding "working definition" of anti-Semitism. I had previously served as a Commissioner on the ASUCD Internal Affairs Commission, debating and approving bills that changed ASUCD internal practices.
